# Personas: AI4Kids

These personas reflect the Vietnam-first beachhead. Interviews and paid-pilot behavior must replace assumptions.

## Primary User: Linh, The Fast-Moving Student

Linh is 12, attends an urban middle school or learning center, and shares a family tablet. She has tried public chatbots and sees AI as a shortcut, creative partner, and search tool.

### Goals

- Finish school tasks faster without getting in trouble.
- Produce work she is proud to show.
- Understand how to get useful answers.
- Earn independence from adults.

### Pain Points

- Classroom AI rules vary by teacher.
- A confident answer looks trustworthy.
- She does not know what counts as cheating versus acceptable help.
- Safety lectures feel unrelated to the task she wants to complete.
- She may share personal context to improve an answer.

### Behaviors To Change

- Make an initial attempt before asking AI.
- State the role AI played.
- Verify important claims.
- Remove identifying data.
- Ask a human when the topic involves safety, health, strong emotions, or major decisions.

### Moment That Matters

Linh catches an AI error, improves the output with her own reasoning, and can show exactly what she contributed.

## Primary Operator: Ms. Thao, The Time-Poor Teacher

Thao teaches English, science, or digital skills to classes of 30-45 students. Leadership expects innovation, but she has received little practical AI training and is worried about academic integrity.

### Jobs

- Run a useful AI lesson without becoming an AI expert.
- Apply one consistent rule across a full class.
- See evidence of student thinking without reading every chat.
- Explain AI use to parents and school leadership.
- Handle low connectivity and shared devices.

### Pain Points

- Creating lessons and rubrics takes too long.
- It is difficult to distinguish learning from answer-copying.
- Policies are vague or absent.
- Open chatbots produce unpredictable content.
- New dashboards often create more work than they remove.

### Purchase Influence

High. Thao may not own the budget, but teacher adoption determines renewal.

### Moment That Matters

She launches a lesson in under 10 minutes and sees, at a glance, which students attempted, checked, revised, and need help.

## Economic Buyer: Mr. Nam, The School Leader

Nam is a principal, academic director, or private learning-center owner. He competes on educational quality and parent trust.

### Goals

- Offer a visible, modern AI program before competitors.
- Reduce cheating, privacy, and reputation risks.
- Give teachers a consistent implementation model.
- Demonstrate measurable student outcomes to parents.
- Avoid expensive custom software or consulting.

### Buying Criteria

- Easy pilot and fast teacher onboarding.
- Vietnamese localization and age-appropriate curriculum.
- Clear privacy and acceptable-use standards.
- Parent-facing evidence and outcome reporting.
- Predictable annual pricing.

### Objections

- "Can teachers do this with free ChatGPT lessons?"
- "Will this create more screen time or parent complaints?"
- "How is this different from VioEdu or Khanmigo?"
- "Will students still cheat outside the platform?"

### Moment That Matters

The pilot produces a board-ready outcome report and parent-facing AI literacy certificate, with teachers asking to continue.

## Trust Buyer: Mai, The Anxious but Ambitious Parent

Mai invests heavily in education and wants her child prepared for an AI-shaped future. She also fears dependency, harmful content, weak critical thinking, and job displacement.

### Needs

- Plain-language rules about what the product does and never does.
- Proof of independent thinking, not chat transcripts.
- A way to discuss AI without becoming a technical expert.
- Assurance that the product is not an emotional companion.
- Visible progress toward future-ready skills.

### Moment That Matters

Her child explains an AI mistake at dinner and shows how they verified and improved the answer.

## Anti-Persona For The First 12 Months

- Children under 10 who require deeper adult co-regulation.
- Schools seeking a complete subject-learning platform.
- Buyers seeking unrestricted chatbot access.
- Direct-to-consumer families requiring high-cost acquisition and individual support.
- Clinical, counseling, or emotional-companion use cases.
