# Journey Map: AI4Kids

## Buyer Journey

### 1. Trigger

**Events**

- Students are caught submitting AI-generated work.
- Parents ask what the school is doing about AI.
- Leadership announces a digital transformation or STEM initiative.
- A teacher wants to use AI but cannot get policy approval.

**Buyer thought:** "We cannot ban this forever, but we cannot open the door without controls."

**AI4Kids response:** Lead with a 6-week Responsible AI Readiness Pilot, not a broad software demo.

### 2. Evaluate

**Questions**

- Does this prevent misuse or merely teach prompting?
- How much teacher preparation is required?
- Does it work in Vietnamese, on shared devices, and with unstable connectivity?
- What student data is stored?
- How is it different from a tutor or a free chatbot?

**Proof**

- Sample school policy kit.
- One complete 20-minute mission.
- Example AI Use Receipt.
- Privacy and safety architecture.
- Pilot outcome dashboard.

### 3. Pilot

1. Leadership selects grades, teachers, and success criteria.
2. AI4Kids customizes the acceptable-use policy and parent letter.
3. Teachers complete a 45-minute onboarding.
4. Students complete a baseline assessment and four missions.
5. Teachers receive weekly class summaries.
6. Leadership receives an outcome and rollout report.

**Friction to remove**

- No SIS integration.
- No student email required.
- Class code access.
- Scripted mode with printable fallback.
- Under 10 minutes of teacher preparation per mission.

### 4. Convert

**Conversion event:** Leadership approves an annual license for selected grades or programs.

**Decision evidence**

- Student pre/post skill gains.
- Teacher time and satisfaction.
- Parent trust feedback.
- Number of classes completing missions.
- Safety incidents and resolution.

### 5. Expand

- Add grades and campuses.
- Certify internal teacher champions.
- Add school-branded parent workshops.
- Add constrained live-AI practice after safety readiness.
- Sell an annual AI-use policy and curriculum update package.

## Student Learning Journey

### Before Class

Linh joins through a class code. The screen states: "This is a tool, not a person. Do not share private information. You are responsible for the final work."

### Think

She answers one short question or attempts the problem before AI becomes available. This creates a baseline and preserves initiative.

### Ask

She builds a prompt with Goal, Context, and Format. Hints improve the request without solving the task.

### Check

She marks uncertain claims, compares a trusted source, and catches a seeded error.

### Protect

She removes identifying information and chooses a trusted-human response for a high-stakes scenario.

### Show

She revises the output and receives an AI Use Receipt:

- My starting idea.
- What I asked AI to do.
- What I checked.
- What I changed.
- What remains mine to decide.

### Continue Offline

The mission ends with a peer, teacher, or family discussion. Retention is driven by capability and recognition, not prolonged chatbot engagement.

## Teacher Journey

### Prepare

- Choose a mission.
- Review a one-page facilitation guide.
- Select scripted or supervised live mode.
- Share class code.

### Facilitate

- Use a projected three-minute introduction.
- Let students work individually or in pairs.
- Pause for one human discussion.
- Use printable cards if devices or connectivity fail.

### Review

The dashboard shows:

- Completion.
- Pre-AI attempt.
- Verification action.
- Privacy decision.
- Revision quality.
- Students needing follow-up.

It does not show private free-text chat by default.

### Report

The teacher exports a class summary for leadership and a plain-language skill update for parents.

## Retention Loops

- **Teacher:** Less preparation plus usable evidence.
- **Student:** New real-world missions and a growing AI Use Passport.
- **School:** Parent trust, compliance readiness, and benchmark reports.
- **Parent:** Offline conversations and visible independent-thinking gains.

## Churn Risks

- Missions feel like generic digital-safety lectures.
- Receipts become another grading burden.
- Older students find the tone childish.
- Schools cannot connect results to curriculum or parent value.
- Free resources appear "good enough."

## Countermeasures

- Use subject-linked missions with an immediate output.
- Auto-score only structured rubric events.
- Create two age bands: 10-11 and 12-14.
- Provide school-branded reports and policy updates.
- Make implementation and evidence, not content volume, the paid value.
